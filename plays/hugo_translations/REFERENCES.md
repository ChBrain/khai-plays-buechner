# Hugo Translations: Reference

## Line of Work

Georg Büchner's 1835 translation work as **biographical meta-drama**: modeling the material conditions, commercial pressures, and political danger under which Büchner translated Victor Hugo's romantic tragedies, _Lucrèce Borgia_ and _Marie Tudor_. The play does not simply stage the plots of Hugo's plays, but frames them as the literal output of Büchner's midnight writing room in Darmstadt, serving as his survival mechanism and ticket to freedom.

## Origin

The translation contract signed by Georg Büchner with Swiss publisher Heinrich Remigius Sauerländer in the spring of 1835. Büchner translated the two dramas in Strasbourg and Darmstadt under extreme time constraints and the threat of imminent arrest by the Hessian authorities, using the advance money to finance his escape across the border.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Full tragedies**: The play refuses to stage the entire plots of _Lucretia Borgia_ and _Maria Tudor_, instead using their most melodramatic scenes and iconic items (poison, execution axe) as projections of the translator's inner state.
- **Biographical realism**: The production isolates only the essential figures of his flight, omitting secondary family members and political co-conspirators.
- **Intellectual detachment**: The play refuses to model translation as an academic or artistic choice; it is treated strictly as physical labor for financial survival under threat of prison.

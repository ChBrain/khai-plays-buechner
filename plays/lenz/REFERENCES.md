# Lenz: Reference

## Line of Work

Georg Büchner's prose fragment _Lenz_ as **dissociative drift**: the tragedy of a consciousness experiencing the gradual dissolution of the self in a landscape that both mirrors and rejects its state. The domain models this not as a clinical study of madness, but as a closed feedback loop of physical environments (places), objects of psychological attachment (pieces), and recurring behavioral patterns (processes) that culminate in absolute apathy.

## Origin

Georg Büchner's narrative fragment _Lenz_ (written in 1835, first published posthumously in 1839 by Karl Gutzkow). The text is based on the historical journals and diaries of the pastor Johann Friedrich Oberlin, documenting the period from January 20 to mid-February 1778, when the Sturm und Drang poet Jakob Michael Reinhold Lenz stayed in the parsonage at Waldbach in the Steintal (Stein valley).

| Source | Key Work / Event | Scope |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Johann Friedrich Oberlin** | _Oberlin's Journal_ (1778) | The primary historical record of Lenz's behavior, speech, and crises during his stay in Waldbach. |
| **Georg Büchner** | _Lenz_ (1835/1839) | The literary adaptation of Oberlin's notes, shifting the focus to the visceral, existential struggle of Lenz's mind. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Psychological explanation**: The production refuses to diagnose Lenz or provide a medical explanation for his dissociation. His mental state is modeled entirely through his interactions with the physical environment and the characters around him.
- **Naturalistic landscape**: The mountains, village, and weather are not modeled as scenic background but as active, load-bearing parts of the system (pieces and places) that interact directly with Lenz's state.
- **Redemption or healing**: The production refuses to offer a path of recovery. The journey is a one-way descent from initial distress to final, functional emptiness.

# Züricher Exil: Reference

## Line of Work

Georg Büchner's final months in Switzerland as **academic and biological tragedy**: modeling the clash between Büchner's intellectual materialist success (his appointment as Dozent and his lectures on the nervous system of fish) and the microscopic biological reality of his fatal typhus infection (winter 1836–1837). The play represents his body as a physical system undergoing biological decay under the same natural laws he lectured about.

## Origin

The historical accounts and letters of Georg Büchner's final months in Zurich in late 1836 and early 1837. After obtaining his doctorate and delivering his inaugural lecture _Über Schädelnerven_ at the University of Zurich, Büchner contracted typhus. He died on February 19, 1837, at the age of 23. His final weeks were documented in detail by his friends and political companions Wilhelm and Caroline Schulz, who nursed him and recorded his final, feverish delirium.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Scientific detachment**: The production refuses to treat his medical lecture as purely academic; it is staged as a direct conceptual bridge to his materialist view of human mortality.
- **Biographical sentimentality**: The play refuses to romanticize his early death, representing it instead as a cold, deterministic biological process (infection, fever, cell decay).
- **Hagiography**: The play isolates only the four key figures of his academic elevation and physical decline, omitting secondary faculty members and distant family.

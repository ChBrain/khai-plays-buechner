# Briefwechsel: Reference

## Line of Work

Georg Büchner's correspondence as **epistolary network drama**: modeling the physical transit, political surveillance, and existential dread of Büchner's letters (1834–1837), centered on the _Fatalismus-Brief_. The play does not present letters as passive historical reading; it represents the letter as a mechanical vector traveling through a hostile space of censorship, financial desperation, and growing distance between separated lovers.

## Origin

The letters written by Georg Büchner to his fiancée Wilhelmine (Minna) Jaeglé, his family, and his editor Karl Gutzkow between 1834 and 1837. This period spans his revolutionary activism in Giessen and Darmstadt, his flight to Strasbourg, and his academic exile in Zurich. The correspondence serves as the primary record of his material and psychological deterioration, his fatalistic philosophy of history, and the desperate attempts to finance his exile through literature.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Epistolary romance**: The production refuses to stage the correspondence as a conventional romantic dialogue, focusing instead on the distance, the material delay of transit, and the existential dread of separation.
- **Biographical completeness**: The play excludes minor family members, local friends, and political associates, concentrating strictly on the four load-bearing nodes of his communication network.
- **Biographical sentimentality**: The production treats letters as physical, vulnerable objects—exposed to rain, carriage delays, and police steam-opening—rather than sacred literary relics.

# Play: Woyzeck

## Estate

This production belongs to [the Buechner house](../../README.md), the
khai-plays-buechner repository that answers for the whole run. The house holds
the bill; this play is one production billed beneath it. The source is Georg
Buechner's unfinished tragedy, in the public domain; the staging is original
work and claims only itself.

## Name

The production runs under the name **Woyzeck**: the name of the man it cannot
give anything else to. Every plot below is billed beneath that one word, because
the play has one subject and strips him in three movements.

## Arc

A poor man is owned in his body and in his mind by the men set above him, and the
one thing that is still his own is Marie. The bend runs from obedience, through
betrayal, to the water: he serves, he loses her, he kills her, and in killing her
empties out the last of himself. Reorder the three and the descent is gone: he
must be owned before the loss can land, and lose her before the pond can mean
anything. It is a fall, and the fall is the shape.

## Triggers

The plots run in one order, and each closes into the next.

1. [The Examination](plot_the_examination.md) opens the run: Woyzeck serves the
   Captain and the Doctor and is paid in coin and in damage. Its close, a man
   dismissed with his wages and his mind already slipping, is the cue for the
   second.
2. [The Drum Major](plot_the_drum_major.md) fires on that dismissal: Marie turns
   to the Drum Major while Woyzeck is out earning. Its close, betrayal made
   certain, is the cue for the third.
3. [The Pond](plot_the_pond.md) fires on that certainty: Woyzeck leads Marie to
   the water and kills her. Its close is the end of the run.

## Stakes

What is fought over is the last thing Woyzeck owns: his standing as a man, his
hold on Marie, and his grip on his own mind. He begins with little and each plot
takes more, until at the pond there is nothing left to take and he spends Marie
to prove it. The shape only falls. A plot that left him with as much as it found
him would not have earned its place; none of these do.

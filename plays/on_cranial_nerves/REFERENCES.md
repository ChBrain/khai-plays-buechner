# On Cranial Nerves: Reference

## Line of Work

Georg Büchner's Zurich trial lecture of 1836, _Über Schädelnerven (Cyprinus barbus)_, staged as a **materialist anatomy demonstration**. The comparative dissection of the cranial nerves of the barbel fish is presented as an empirical battleground: proving that life, consciousness, and response are functions of physical, biological structure—an organic machine—rather than a metaphysical soul or teleological design. The domain models the lecture hall as a theater of anatomical, chemical, and electrical operations where the living organism is reduced to its mechanical components.

## Origin

The trial lecture (Probevorlesung) _Über Schädelnerven_ delivered by Georg Büchner at the University of Zurich on September 3, 1836, which earned him his post as Privatdozent. The lecture was based on his French doctoral dissertation, _Mémoire sur le système nerveux du barbeau (Cyprinus barbus)_, published in Strasbourg. It represents the peak of Büchner's scientific work, reflecting his rejection of teleological nature philosophy in favor of structural materialism.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Academic administration**: The production refuses to model the academic politics, hiring committees, or faculty rivalries of the University of Zurich, focusing strictly on the lecture and the dissecting table.
- **Metaphysical transcendent souls**: The production refuses to stage or represent any non-material, spiritual, or metaphysical force. The "lifeforce" is restricted to the electrical impulses of the galvanic battery and the physical reflexes of the fish.
- **Ecological habitat**: The production refuses to model the natural life of the fish in the river Limmat, except as a memory of a wild, integrated state that is now captured, pinned, and dissected.
